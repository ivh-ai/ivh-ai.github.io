How to use

Playing MathSprint

90 seconds on the clock, as many questions as you can answer. Pick an operation, build a streak, and chase the leaderboard. Here's everything you need to know.

Quick start

Pick a mode (say, Addition) → enter your name → hit Start Game → tap the right answer, again and again, until the buzzer.

Step by step

  1. Choose a game mode

    The home screen offers four: Addition, Subtraction, Multiplication, and Division. Each is its own separate challenge with its own leaderboard — so you can be the addition champion even if times tables aren't your thing.

  2. Read the rules, enter your name

    The instructions screen recaps how the round works and asks for your name — that's what appears on the leaderboard if you post a score. Then hit Start Game. The 90-second clock starts immediately.

  3. Answer fast — but answer once

    Each question (like 12 + 4) shows three answer choices. Tap the right one and your score ticks up. Here's the catch: you get one attempt per question. A wrong tap still moves you to the next question — there's no second chance, so careless speed costs points.

  4. Use the keyboard

    On a computer, press 1, 2, or 3 to pick the first, second, or third answer. It's meaningfully faster than the mouse — most top scores are keyboard runs.

  5. Watch your streak

    The header tracks seconds left, score, question number, and your streak — consecutive correct answers. Streaks are the real skill meter: anyone can go fast, but a long streak means you're fast and accurate. Your best streak shows on the results screen.

  6. See your results and the leaderboard

    When time's up you get the full summary — score, questions answered, best streak — and your score posts to that mode's shared leaderboard. Check the Leaderboard tab anytime to see the top scores in each of the four modes.

Getting a high score

Every correct answer is one point, so the math is simple: answer more, miss less. The three answer choices are deliberately close together (off-by-a-little traps, not obviously wrong numbers), so the winning rhythm is a half-second of actual checking rather than pure reflex tapping. Play all four modes — division runs are where leaderboards are usually softest.
